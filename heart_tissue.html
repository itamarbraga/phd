Statistical methodology & data integrity notice

Outlier handling: No datapoints are removed from this analysis. Outlier removal in small clinical datasets (n = 10–21 per group) is not appropriate unless a confirmed technical failure (sequencing error, sample contamination) is documented. Biological extreme values reflect genuine inter-individual variability. Statistical outliers detected by the 1.5×IQR rule are ⚠ flagged in-table and reported in sensitivity analyses; all reach the same qualitative conclusions with or without the flagged observation. Non-parametric Mann-Whitney U and Kruskal-Wallis tests are inherently robust to extreme values through rank transformation.

Small subgroup policy: Subgroups with n = 3–4 are marked as having limited power and results are interpreted cautiously. Subgroups with n = 1–2 are marked as statistically non-viable — no inferential testing is performed and values are reported as descriptive data points only. This applies to most cancer-type and DCM gene-level subgroups.

Multiple comparisons: All disease groups are compared individually to the Healthy reference group (no inter-disease group comparisons). No Bonferroni or FDR correction is applied — this is an exploratory analysis. All p-values should be interpreted accordingly and are presented to facilitate hypothesis generation.

Section 1
Study Design & Sample Description

This is a targeted mitochondrial DNA sequencing study of left ventricular (LV) snap-frozen biopsy tissue obtained at the time of LVAD implantation for end-stage heart failure (Cardiotoxic and DCM groups), at the time of heart transplantation or donation (Healthy group), and from an in vitro iPSC-derived cardiomyocyte model. All Cardiotoxic biopsies represent end-stage anthracycline-induced HF with LVEF 0.10–10% at implantation, providing a unique and clinically meaningful tissue resource.

Critical negative finding — in vitro vs human tissue dissociation Doxorubicin treatment at cytotoxic concentrations (up to MTT50 ≈ 50% cell death) produces no detectable change in mtDNA copy number, SNV rate, indel rate, or total mutation burden in CTP2-C5 cardiomyocytes. This contrasts sharply with the elevated parameters seen in human Cardiotoxic heart tissue. Possible explanations include: (1) acute vs chronic exposure dynamics — tissue represents decades of accumulated damage; (2) absence of systemic co-factors (neurohormonal, inflammatory, hemodynamic); (3) iPSC-CM metabolic immaturity; (4) in vivo selective pressure on damaged cardiomyocytes. This dissociation strengthens the argument that the human LVAD tissue captures unique biology inaccessible by current in vitro models.

Section 14
Integrated Summary & Cardio-Oncology Relevance
Principal findings ranked by statistical strength
Elevated mtDNA copy number in Cardiotoxic vs Healthy (p = 0.026*). Biogenesis upregulation as compensatory response to anthracycline-induced oxidative stress.
Reduced mtDNA copy number in DCM gen. mutation vs Healthy (p = 0.029*). Primary mitochondrial depletion — mechanistically opposite to Cardiotoxic.
Elevated indels in DCM gen. mutation vs Healthy (p = 0.004**). The strongest individual finding. Elevated indel burden despite low copy number points to replication fidelity compromise without compensatory biogenesis.
Elevated SNVs and mutation burden in Cardiotoxic vs Healthy (p = 0.039* for both). ROS-driven point mutation accumulation in anthracycline-damaged myocardium.
Elevated SNVs and mutation burden in DCM nonmutant vs Healthy (p = 0.018* and 0.022*). Oxidative mtDNA accumulation in non-genetic cardiomyopathy.
Progressive indel accumulation over time in Cardiotoxic: Slow-interval patients (≥ 36 months) show significantly elevated indels vs Healthy (p = 0.035*). Suggests continued mtDNA instability years after chemotherapy.
No dose-response between cumulative doxorubicin and any mtDNA parameter. After controlling for time-to-LVAD (which is confounded with dose), dox dose has no independent effect. Individual susceptibility, not dose, determines mitochondrial damage.
In vitro doxorubicin does not recapitulate human tissue mtDNA signature. Complete absence of mtDNA parameter changes even at MTT50 (50% viability). Systemic in vivo factors essential for the tissue phenotype.

Section 15
Limitations
  • Sample size: n = 10–21 per group limits statistical power. Findings are hypothesis-generating; replication in larger cohorts is required before clinical translation.
  • Single timepoint (end-stage): All disease samples represent end-stage HF at LVAD implantation. Longitudinal sampling would be required to determine trajectory and causality.
  • No heteroplasmy quantification: Summary-level variant rates without allele frequency data prevent distinction between homoplasmic and heteroplasmic variants — critical for mechanistic interpretation.
  • Copy number and NUMTs: MT/autosomal read ratio may be influenced by nuclear mitochondrial DNA segments (NUMTs), particularly in damaged tissue. Confirmatory ddPCR is recommended.
  • Healthy group heterogeneity: HTX biopsies and DCD donors are combined; DCD samples may carry acute ischaemia-reperfusion effects on mtDNA.
  • Cardiotoxicity definition — post hoc: "Cardiotoxic" is defined retrospectively by LVAD need, capturing only the most severe end of the anthracycline cardiotoxicity spectrum.
  • VUS classification: Three Cardiotoxic patients carry FLNC/MYL3 VUS. Reclassification of FLNC variants as pathogenic would change group assignment for patients 5 and 7.

Revised hypothesis — phenotypic reframing The "sensitive phenotype" is better characterised as individuals with a low heteroplasmy threshold who reach pathogenic mtDNA dysfunction rapidly after any anthracycline exposure, regardless of dose. They present clinically as rapid progressors (LVAD within 1–4 years). Their elevated copy number reflects an acute biogenesis stress response — a marker of rapid cardiac compromise, not a dose-damage relationship. High-dose patients are better characterised as the resilient phenotype: sufficient mitochondrial reserve to tolerate higher cumulative doses while delaying clinical decompensation by 5–38 years. Individual susceptibility — likely genetic, epigenetic, or metabolic — not dose, is the primary determinant.
